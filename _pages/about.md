# About Me
Hi! I am Jia, a Doctoral Candidate in Communication at the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign. Before continuing into the doctoral program, I obtained my Bachelor’s degree in Communication and Psychology, and my Master’s degree in Communication, both from Illinois.

I study **interpersonal and health communication**. My research is guided by a central question: How can communication in personal relationships and healthcare settings reduce the burden of illness, support better health decision-making, and improve well-being? I focus on how communication about health challenges, and responses to support and advice, are shaped by people’s sense of identity. My research revolves around two core areas, which together examine how people seek, interpret, and respond to health-related communication.

My primary area of research focuses on **supportive communication**. I examine how people navigate social support for illness and health challenges within close relationships. Concerns about stigma, burden, or relational strain often make it difficult to express support needs. I study how these barriers are experienced across different populations and relational contexts, and how they shape the ways people seek support and communicate distress. My recent projects explore topics such as how cultural and relational contexts shape mental health disclosure and support seeking, the support-seeking struggles faced by young adult cancer patients, and the role of verbal person-centeredness and nonverbal cues in supporting individuals with invisible illness.

My second area examines **how expert communication translates medical information into actionable patient understanding**. I investigate how communication strategies used by experts shape patient interpretation, trust, and health behaviors. In this work, I consider both who the message comes from and how the message itself is presented and understood. My research aims to identify how expert communication can be designed to foster understanding, support patient autonomy, and reduce resistance to medical advice. My recent projects in this area examine how message source can reduce resistance to health recommendations, how physicians use analogies to explain medical information (e.g., describing the immune system as a sports team), and how nonverbal communication shapes patient–provider interactions.

Across these areas, my research advances a broader goal of understanding how communication processes shape how people make sense of health challenges and how they respond to support and influence. By connecting interpersonal communication and social influence perspectives, my work contributes to theory while also informing practical approaches to improving health and well-being.
